# 01. Why Software Needs Life

## Starting observation

Software has traditionally been treated as a machine designed by humans and
completed through delivery. That model remains useful for deterministic
components, but it is incomplete for systems that operate continuously inside
changing social and technical environments.

AI changes the economic boundary. Producing a local code change is becoming
cheaper. Preserving purpose, coherence, trust, memory, and recoverability across
many generated changes becomes more important.

ODD therefore explores another model:

> Software is not merely assembled. It is cultivated under continuous exposure
> to an environment.

## What “life” means here

ODD does not attempt to settle the scientific definition of life or claim that a
program is biologically alive. “Life” names a bundle of engineering capabilities:

- sensing internal and external state;
- maintaining viable operating ranges;
- retaining useful memory;
- containing damage and recovering;
- adapting behavior through feedback;
- coordinating specialized parts;
- evolving without losing accountable identity.

## Working hypothesis

If these capabilities are designed as a connected system rather than isolated
features, software can remain useful under change with less unrecognized damage
and less dependence on heroic human intervention.

This hypothesis must be tested against ordinary automation and conventional
reliability engineering. A biological vocabulary is valuable only if it reveals
signals, relationships, or interventions that those approaches miss.
